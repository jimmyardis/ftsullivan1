Sullivan's Island, South Carolina

Fort Sullivan

A Masterpiece of Place

Est. 1891

Sullivan's Island  ·  South Carolina

Sullivan's Island's only private castle.

Situated on the western end of Sullivan's Island, Fort Sullivan is a one-of-a-kind private castle and bastion of hospitality on one of the most storied barrier islands on the East Coast. Guests arrive to find a Medieval Gothic chapel built in 1891, a Romanesque Keep completed in 2010, and more than a century of remarkable history folded into rubble granite walls, pointed arches, and crenelated parapets overlooking Charleston Harbor. It is a place that has served soldiers, congregations, and now the rare few fortunate enough to call it home for a night.

Est. 1891 · Keep completed 2010 · 1401 Middle Street, Sullivan's Island, S.C.
Chapter I

The Chapel 1891

On the 12th of September, 1891, a cornerstone was laid in ceremony on the western end of Sullivan's Island. The building that rose from that rubble granite foundation was called the Chapel of the Holy Cross — designed by New York architect W. W. Deveaux in the Medieval Gothic tradition, built for just over $6,000 by Charleston contractor Robert McCarrel.

The stone walls were built thick enough to hold a century's worth of whispers — and they did. Deeply recessed pointed-arch windows threw long shadows across the nave. A corner bell tower, original to the build, gave the modest mission chapel the unmistakable silhouette of something older and more permanent than its modest budget suggested.

The island had been without its own Episcopal sanctuary for decades. An earlier church had been shelled into ruin by Union batteries on Morris Island during the Civil War. The Chapel of the Holy Cross was the island exhaling.

Scottish Heritage

Mugdock

The inspiration for Fort Sullivan's transformation reaches across the Atlantic to Mugdock Castle in Stirlingshire, Scotland — seat of Clan Graham since the 13th century. The same rubble granite construction, the same spirit of a place built to outlast its builders. Inside these walls, that lineage is felt in the fireplace, the library, the great hall.

"The stone nave was bare, its tall ceiling carried by open timber trusses and hammer beams — light entering only through the deep reveals of pointed-arch windows."
— Witold Rybczynski, The Pennsylvania Gazette

The name Fort Sullivan carries an echo that reaches across the Atlantic. Mugdock Castle — seat of Clan Graham since the 13th century — shares both the rubble granite construction and the spirit of a place built to outlast its builders. The transformation into a private castle is, in some sense, a homecoming.

Interlude

The Conversion

In 1902, the United States government moved to condemn the Chapel of the Holy Cross. Fort Moultrie was expanding its coastal defenses — Endicott-era batteries, disappearing artillery carriages, minefields and rapid-fire guns pointed toward the harbor mouth. The chapel stood in the way of a militarized landscape.

The trustees agreed to sell. The compensation, $9,000, changed hands over several years. The keys were finally transferred in 1907. The stained glass memorial windows, the silver communion service, the chancel beams — the congregation took what they could to a new Chapel at 2520 Middle Street and held their first service there in June of 1908.

For the next forty years, the stone building on Middle Street served as a post chapel for the soldiers of Fort Moultrie. Weddings were held here in wartime. The rough granite walls absorbed their prayers the same way they had absorbed the Episcopal liturgy before them.

When the fort was deactivated in 1947, the building passed through a quiet relay: from the War Assets Administration to a private buyer named Bischoff, then to the Town, then to the South Carolina Lutheran Synod. A Lutheran congregation worshipped in these same stones until 1972, when they relocated to Isle of Palms and became St. Mark's. The chapel was deconsecrated. For the first time in eighty years, the building had no congregation to serve.

Chapter II

The Evolution

In 2002, a plan to relocate the entire structure to the I'On development in Mount Pleasant was floated — and blocked by local preservation action. The building stayed. And from that decision, something remarkable began.

The architects of New World Byzantine were engaged. Their mandate was unusual: design an addition that looked older than the Gothic church it was joining. The Romanesque tradition was chosen deliberately — the rounded arch as answer to the pointed arch, stone answering stone across a century's difference.

Chapter III

The Sanctuary 2010

The Keep was completed in 2010. Reinforced concrete walls, massive cypress timbers, a geothermal heat pump hidden beneath the old stone floors. From the street it reads as something ancient — crenelated parapets, Romanesque arched openings, a tower profile that looks like it predates the chapel rather than postdates it. That was the intention.

The upper portion of the original bell tower had been removed at some point in the military era; crenelations were installed in its place, beginning the fortress profile that the Keep would complete. By 2010, the transformation was total. Fort Sullivan had become something that had never existed before: a private castle on a Carolina barrier island.
