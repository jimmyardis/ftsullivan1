Sullivan's Island  ·  South Carolina

A Soulful Place of Awe and Wonder.

A romantic and whimsical fortified home on the western end of Sullivan's Island, Fort Sullivan guards the entrance to Charleston Harbor alongside Forts Sumter and Moultrie — the most historic North American port of the Atlantic Coast. Two structures fused into one: a Medieval Gothic chapel built in 1891, and a Romanesque keep completed in 2010, joined by rubble granite, sea air, and two centuries of accumulated time made stone.

Est. 1776 · Chapel 1891 · Keep 2010 · 1401 Middle Street, Sullivan's Island, S.C.
The Foundation

Fort Sullivan 1776

On July 28th, 1776, Colonel William Moultrie and 435 brave Americans turned back Admiral Peter Parker and nine warships of the powerful British Navy at the very ground where Fort Sullivan now stands. It was the first decisive victory of the War for Independence — helping win uncommitted Americans to the struggle for liberty and keeping Charleston, the largest city in the South, free from British occupation.

The fort that bore this island's name — and this castle's name — predates the Republic itself. Sullivan's Island had been a threshold long before that battle: the Ellis Island of the African diaspora, the quarantine station through which hundreds of thousands of enslaved Africans passed before dispersal across the American South. Their arrival shaped the Lowcountry's language, its food, its music, its soul. The ground beneath Fort Sullivan has been absorbing history for three centuries.

Combine African and European cultures with a semi-tropical climate, and the smell of salt marsh and oysters, and you get the exotic Lowcountry. Shakespeare said "There is a tide in the affairs of men" — and nowhere is this better felt than in the sensual rhythm, ever changing, of Lowcountry life.

Chapter I

The Winter Hall 1891

On the 12th of September, 1891, a cornerstone was laid in ceremony on the western end of Sullivan's Island. The building that rose from that rubble granite foundation was called the Chapel of the Holy Cross — designed by New York architect W. W. Deveaux in the Medieval Gothic tradition, built for just over $6,000 by Charleston contractor Robert McCarrel. Two-foot-thick Georgia granite walls. A corner bell tower. Pointed-arch windows throwing long shadows across the nave.

Today the Gothic structure is known as the Winter Hall: the kitchen, the dining hall, the library, the buttery, the throne room, and the minstrel gallery all find their home in these ancient walls. The North Tower houses a small bedchamber, with the casemate above reached by ship's ladder from the ramparts.

The island had been without its own Episcopal sanctuary for decades. An earlier church had been shelled into ruin by Union batteries on Morris Island during the Civil War. The Chapel of the Holy Cross was the island exhaling. The stone walls were built thick enough to hold a century's worth of whispers — and they did.

"The stone nave was bare, its tall ceiling carried by open timber trusses and hammer beams — light entering only through the deep reveals of pointed-arch windows."
— Witold Rybczynski, The Pennsylvania Gazette
The Mugdock Decree
"Strive for the luxury of expert simplicity reflective of the comfort and utility of a modern day beach castle. The choice of finishings, furniture, and other accoutrements must reflect a time when understated elegance was still an art form. Incorporate no superfluous objects. Instead, with each addition or alteration, aim for a soulful harmony such that the whole is made ever greater than the sum of its parts. Achieve value through thoughtful selection, rather than accumulation."
Scottish Heritage

Mugdock

The name Fort Sullivan carries an echo that reaches across the Atlantic. Mugdock Castle in Stirlingshire, Scotland — seat of Clan Graham since the 13th century — shares both the rubble granite construction and the spirit of a place built to outlast its builders. The men and women responsible for the restoration of the Winter Hall and the construction of the Summer Hall are skilled artisans hailing from throughout Europe and the Americas. Passion for their craft is reflected in the heart and soul thrown into every detail.

Interlude

The Conversion

In 1902, the United States government moved to condemn the Chapel of the Holy Cross. Fort Moultrie was expanding its coastal defenses — Endicott-era batteries, disappearing artillery carriages, minefields and rapid-fire guns pointed toward the harbor mouth. The chapel stood in the way of a militarized landscape.

The trustees agreed to sell. The compensation, $9,000, changed hands over several years. The keys were finally transferred in 1907. The stained glass memorial windows, the silver communion service, the chancel beams — the congregation took what they could to a new Chapel at 2520 Middle Street and held their first service there in June of 1908.

For the next forty years, the stone building on Middle Street served as a post chapel for the soldiers of Fort Moultrie. Weddings were held here in wartime. The rough granite walls absorbed their prayers the same way they had absorbed the Episcopal liturgy before them.

When the fort was deactivated in 1947, the building passed through a quiet relay: from the War Assets Administration to a private buyer named Bischoff, then to the Town, then to the South Carolina Lutheran Synod. A Lutheran congregation worshipped in these same stones until 1972, when they relocated to Isle of Palms and became St. Mark's. The chapel was deconsecrated. For the first time in eighty years, the building had no congregation to serve.

Chapter II

The Evolution

In 2002, a plan to relocate the entire structure to the I'On development in Mount Pleasant was floated — and blocked by local preservation action. The building stayed. And from that decision, something remarkable began.

The architects of New World Byzantine were engaged. Their mandate was unusual: design an addition that looked older than the Gothic church it was joining. The Romanesque tradition was chosen deliberately — the rounded arch as answer to the pointed arch, stone answering stone across a century's difference.

Chapter III

The Summer Hall 2010

The Norman Keep — the Summer Hall — was completed in 2010. Battered masonry walls two and a half feet thick at the base, massive cypress timbers, reinforced concrete, a geothermal heat pump plunging into the same Sullivan's Island ground that Robert McCarrel broke in 1891. From the street it reads as something ancient — crenelated parapets, Romanesque arched openings, a tower profile that looks like it predates the chapel it joins rather than postdating it. That was the intention.

The Summer Hall houses the undercroft, the castle laundry, six bedchambers with associated wardrobes, and five and a half garderobes. Two south-facing porches provide access to additional dining facilities atop the southern ramparts. Above it all, the Morning Courtyard to the east and the Summer Lawn to the south give the grounds the quiet grandeur of a place designed not for display, but for living.

"Designed to look older than the church it joined — the Romanesque as an answer to the Gothic, stone answering stone across a century's silence."
— New World Byzantine Architecture
